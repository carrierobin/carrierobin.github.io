Dialysis in America, 2025

Artificial kidneys in a post-COVID world

By Carrie Arnold

More than half a million Americans rely on dialysis to stay alive. Three times each week, those with kidney failure must travel to a dialysis clinic to have their blood cleaned by machines that do what their kidneys cannot. The process is exhausting, time-consuming, and expensive. Those with kidney failure represent just 1% of Medicare recipients in the U.S., but account for 7% of Medicare spending. Reining in this spending has been a challenge for more than a half century.

Once someone's kidneys fail, they have three options: start dialysis, get a kidney transplant, or let nature take its course. Without adequate kidneys for transplant, this means that 560,000 of the more than 810,000 Americans currently living with kidney failurerequire thrice-weekly dialysis to stay alive. Nearly all those with kidney failure automatically qualify for government-funded Medicare coverage, and several large dialysis companies currently control most of the American market--and its profits. As a result, the U.S. has more free-standing dialysis clinics than many fast food chains.

All of these clinics, however, are not created equal. Some have just a few chairs, while the largest dialysis clinic has as many as 80. They also vary widely in quality. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), which regulates dialysis care in the U.S., assigns each clinic a quality rating on a 5-point scale. CMS said that the quality ratings will allow patients to select the best clinic, but the organization hasn't shared documentation about how well the program works.

The Covid-19 pandemic has triggered massive changes in how dialysis care is delivered. Other countries such as Singapore and Costa Rica have long relied on training patients to perform dialysis at home, but the approach never really gained popularity in the U.S. Pandemic-related lockdowns and concerns arrived on the back of a 2019 executive order meant to bolster home-based dialysis and kidney transplant.

Despite early enthusiasm about the 2019 Executive Order, recent results show that the document had minimal impact on kidney care delivery. In large swaths of the country, no dialysis clinics are able to train patients to perform treatments at home.

Other parts of the country lack nearby dialysis facilities, forcing patients either to pack up and move or to drive long distances. A 2024 paper in the Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology documented a net loss of 215 dialysis clinics from 2021 to 2023, or 2.7% of all U.S. facilities.
